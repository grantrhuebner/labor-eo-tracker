Exclusions from Federal Labor-Management Relations Programs
Strips collective bargaining rights from ~800,000 federal workers at 40+ agencies by invoking a broad "national security" exemption. Ends decades of union contracts at VA, EPA, DOJ, DHS, and more. As of Feb 27, 2026, the 9th Circuit vacated the main injunction.

⚡ Plain English: What This Means for Workers The president declared that 40+ federal agencies — including the VA, EPA, and portions of DHS — are involved in "national security," which under a longstanding law strips their employees of union rights. This is the same legal argument Reagan used in 1981 against air traffic controllers, but applied on a vastly larger scale. Nearly 800,000 federal workers lost the right to bargain collectively overnight. In early 2026, the 9th Circuit sided with the administration, meaning the EO is now largely in effect while the underlying legal case continues. A final ruling could go to the Supreme Court.

Core Legal Dispute: Presidential Authority to Strip Federal Union Rights

⚖ Labor / Unions Argue

L
The FSLMRS (the federal labor relations law) sets narrow, specific criteria for national security exemptions. The president cannot unilaterally expand them to cover 40 agencies by executive order.
L
Stripping workers of bargaining rights because of their union membership is viewpoint-based discrimination — a First Amendment violation. The EO targets unions specifically.
L
Congress created collective bargaining rights for federal workers. Only Congress can take them away. The president lacks the unilateral authority to override a statute via EO.

⚖ Administration Argues

G
The president has broad constitutional authority over the executive branch and national security — particularly over who can speak collectively on behalf of agencies in sensitive roles.
G
The FSLMRS explicitly contains a national security exception. The president is the authoritative judge of what constitutes a national security mission.
G
Federal employees work for the executive branch and their employment conditions are ultimately a management prerogative, not a constitutional right.

L

L
The DOD/GSA memos were an unlawful end-run — agencies cannot make de facto policy changes that contradict an existing EO without going through the formal rulemaking or EO rescission process.

G
The president and executive agencies have discretion over how to implement procurement policy. Internal guidance memos are a legitimate management tool.
G
PLAs raise costs for the government, reduce competition by effectively requiring union labor on public projects, and burden non-union contractors who pay taxes like everyone else.
G
Congress's Competition in Contracting Act requires competitive bidding. Mandating PLAs restricts the pool of qualified bidders and drives up costs to taxpayers.

DEI EOs: First Amendment, Vagueness, and Government Speech

⚖ Challengers Argue

L
Requiring contractors to certify they don't participate in DEI programs is viewpoint-based discrimination — the government is conditioning benefits on whether you hold certain views about diversity. That's a First Amendment violation.
L
The term "illegal DEI" is unconstitutionally vague — no reasonable contractor or employer can know what conduct is prohibited, creating a chilling effect on all diversity-related programs.
L
DEI programs help comply with existing civil rights law. Banning them puts employers in an impossible position: comply with the EO or comply with Title VII.

⚖ Administration Argues

G
Race-conscious DEI programs that give preferences based on race are already illegal under Title VI, Title VII, and the Equal Protection Clause. The EO enforces existing law, not new restrictions.
G
The government has broad authority over who it contracts with and can set conditions on federal spending. Contractors aren't compelled to do anything — they can simply decline federal contracts.
G
Race-based preferences harm qualified applicants of all backgrounds and undermine merit-based hiring — the EO protects all workers from discrimination.

⚡ Plain English: What This Means for Workers The Davis-Bacon Act requires that workers on federally funded construction projects be paid the "prevailing wage" in the area — historically, the union wage. The Trump DOL is attacking this from two angles: (1) cutting specific regional wage determinations, like at Cape Canaveral, replacing higher union rates with lower area averages; and (2) freezing the process for updating those rates when union contracts negotiate higher wages. The freeze announced in February 2026 means that even when IBEW Local 11 negotiates a raise, that raise won't count when calculating the federal wage floor — effectively pricing union labor out of federal contracts.

Key Actions & Dates

Jul 4, 2025
Cape Canaveral/Kennedy Space Center wage determination cut. Union-negotiated rates replaced with lower Brevard County averages. SMART Union files formal protest.
Feb 19, 2026
DOL announces CBA rate updates to Davis-Bacon determinations are "delayed" — freezing union-negotiated wages out of federal contract bidding calculations across the country.
Ongoing
2023 Davis-Bacon Modernization Rule (which expanded coverage and updated calculation methodology) faces ongoing court challenges that Trump DOL may now abandon to defend.

Why Davis-Bacon Matters

The Mechanism
When DOL lowers a wage determination, every contractor bidding federal work can underbid union contractors on labor costs — directly undermining collective bargaining and creating downward wage pressure across the entire construction market.
The CBA Freeze
When DOL stops incorporating CBA rates into wage determinations, IBEW's negotiated raises become irrelevant to federal contract bids — breaking the link between collective bargaining and federal prevailing wages.
⚡ Critical IBEW Local 11 Impact Davis-Bacon prevailing wage rates for Los Angeles and Southern California directly set the wage floor for all IBEW Local 11 members working federal jobs — military bases, federal buildings, post offices, infrastructure. Rate rollbacks could undercut Local 11's ability to enforce standard wages on federal work and weaken bargaining leverage across the region. The CBA rate freeze is a particularly serious long-term threat.

Davis-Bacon CBA Rate Freeze: Legal vs. Policy Authority

⚖ Labor / Challengers Would Argue

L
The Davis-Bacon Act requires DOL to survey and publish prevailing wages for construction workers, including collectively bargained rates. A policy of ignoring CBA rates violates the statute's plain requirements.
L
The DOL "delay" constitutes unlawful agency inaction — courts can compel agencies to perform non-discretionary statutory duties under the Administrative Procedure Act.
L
Cutting wage determinations without required public notice-and-comment rulemaking violates the APA's procedural requirements for changing established agency policy.

⚖ Administration Would Argue

G
DOL has discretion over timing and methodology for updating wage determinations. An administrative delay in processing is within the agency's operational authority.
G
The "prevailing wage" should reflect actual market wages, not union-negotiated rates that may be artificially high relative to what nonunion workers earn in the same area.
G
Davis-Bacon has consistently been found to raise costs on federal construction projects. Recalibrating wage determinations saves taxpayers money and increases competition.

The Davis-Bacon rollbacks are a sleeper issue that directly affects IBEW Local 11's bottom line on every federal job. Urgent action recommended.

⚡ Plain English: What This Means for Workers On his first day in office, Trump signed an EO revoking 78 Biden executive actions. For labor, the most consequential was the "nondisplacement" rule — which required that when a federal service contract changes hands (e.g., a facilities management contract at a federal building is rebid), the new contractor had to keep the previous contractor's workers employed. This protected unionized workforces from being replaced wholesale when contract renewals came up. It is now gone, and no court has blocked it.

Key Rescissions for Labor

EO 14055
Nondisplacement of workers on federal service contracts. Required successor contractors to retain predecessor workers. Rescinded. New contractors can now replace entire unionized workforces.
EO 14126
Investing in America and Investing in American Workers — prioritized PLAs, registered apprenticeships, and union neutrality on federally funded infrastructure. Rescinded.
Related
Registered apprenticeship program requirements for certain federal contracts also eliminated — weakening the pipeline that connects IBEW apprenticeship programs to federal work.

Legal Outlook

Litigation Status
No injunction has been sought or granted. Pre-existing litigation over Biden's nondisplacement rule is winding down — now largely academic since the EO is gone.
Path Forward
Restoration of these protections would require either a new executive order (next administration) or an Act of Congress codifying nondisplacement and apprenticeship requirements into law.
⚡ IBEW Relevance The nondisplacement rule protected IBEW members at facilities with long-term service contracts. Its rescission means that when federal facilities contracts (maintenance, operations) are rebid, contractors can eliminate IBEW workers entirely and hire non-union replacements — a significant tool for union-busting on federal work.

Nondisplacement: Could It Have Been Defended?

⚖ Case For Nondisplacement

L
Continuity of service workforce reduces transition costs and maintains institutional knowledge — particularly relevant at technical federal facilities (power plants, research facilities) where IBEW members work.
L
Without nondisplacement, contractors compete on labor cost alone at rebid — creating a race to the bottom that undermines wage standards and quality of service on federal contracts.
L
The president has broad authority over federal procurement policy. Biden had a strong legal basis for the rule under the Federal Property and Administrative Services Act.

⚖ Case Against Nondisplacement

G
Nondisplacement effectively entrenches existing workforces and removes the competitive benefit of contractor transitions — the new contractor can't bring in its own team or management approach.
G
The Competition in Contracting Act requires competitive procurement. Requiring retention of a specific workforce limits a new contractor's operational flexibility.
G
Presidents have broad discretion over federal procurement policy in both directions — what Biden could create, Trump could rescind.

With no injunction possible and the rule gone, the focus shifts to legislative preservation and contract-level protections.

Reinstates "Schedule F" — enabling reclassification of career federal employees as at-will workers removable without cause. Combined with DOGE-driven mass terminations, attacks the civil service infrastructure that enforces Davis-Bacon, OSHA, and NLRB labor protections.

⚡ Plain English: What This Means for Workers "Schedule F" allows the president to reclassify career government employees who do "policy-related" work as at-will workers — meaning they can be fired without cause. In practice, this could cover a large swath of the career civil service. Paired with mass terminations of probationary employees, this is the largest attack on the career civil service in modern history. The indirect impact on IBEW is significant: the career federal employees who enforce Davis-Bacon wage compliance, conduct OSHA inspections, and staff NLRB field offices are the regulators that protect union standards — and they're being fired.

Key Timeline

Jan 20, 2025
Schedule F EO reinstated on day one. Combined with mandatory return-to-office and DOGE-directed RIFs across agencies.
Feb–Mar 2025
Mass terminations of tens of thousands of probationary federal employees. Multiple courts issue TROs with conflicting scope and outcomes.
Ongoing
Cases in multiple circuits. MSPB, FLRA, and federal courts all have jurisdiction over different aspects. No single definitive ruling on Schedule F's constitutionality yet.

The Regulatory Infrastructure Risk

Davis-Bacon
DOL Wage and Hour Division investigators who enforce Davis-Bacon compliance have been subject to RIFs. Fewer investigators = less enforcement of prevailing wage requirements on federal construction.
OSHA
OSHA inspectors have faced significant layoffs. Reduced inspection capacity means fewer safety inspections on construction sites — union and non-union.
NLRB
NLRB regional offices have faced staff reductions and leadership changes. Slower case processing and weakened unfair labor practice enforcement.
⚡ IBEW Relevance — Regulatory Infrastructure This is the sleeper threat: even if IBEW wins specific EO legal battles, gutting the career civil service eliminates the enforcement apparatus that makes labor law mean something. Schedule F attacks the regulators who enforce Davis-Bacon, OSHA safety standards, and NLRB decisions that protect IBEW members every day.

Schedule F: Can the President Override Congressional Civil Service Law?

⚖ Unions / Challengers Argue

L
The Civil Service Reform Act (1978) created specific, congressionally mandated protections for federal employees. An executive order cannot override a statute — only Congress can undo what Congress created.
L
Schedule F is unconstitutionally vague: defining "policy-related" work broadly enough to cover most of the civil service gives the president unchecked power to politicize the entire executive branch.
L
An independent civil service is constitutionally implied — if the president can fire anyone at will, the separation of powers protections that give agencies independent authority are undermined.

⚖ Administration Argues

G
Article II of the Constitution gives the president the executive power and the authority to demand accountability from executive branch employees. The president cannot perform the executive function if he cannot control the people who implement policy.
G
An at-will workforce is more responsive to democratic accountability — voters elect the president to implement a specific agenda, which requires a workforce willing to carry it out.
G
Congress's civil service protections can be understood as applying to the general civil service, while Schedule F creates a limited category of senior policy-influencing roles exempt from those protections.

The Schedule F / civil service assault threatens the enforcement infrastructure for all labor law. Recommended actions focus on protecting regulatory capacity and supporting fired workers.
